Tiger Paradise 🗻🗻🗻

Tigers are some of the most majestic (and largest!) big cats found around the world, often seen as the most beautiful big cat, the tiger is also one of the most endangered cats in the wild, with some subspecies of tiger already being completely killed off by human poaching or other causes, and the others dwindling in numbers at an alarming rate. Below you will find a selection of the six tiger subspecies found in the wild!


Bengal Tigers

Bengal Tigers are often regarded as the largest wild cats alive, residing in humid forests in India, Bangladesh, Nepal and Bhutan. Bengals have a coat yellowish to light orange, with stripes ranging from dark brown to black, the belly is white and the tail is orange with black rings. Although Bengals have been known to have several "mutations", such as white or pale golden fur (golden tiger) these are extremely rare to the point the former has only one confirmed sighting within the wild. Although like all Tiger subspecies the Bengal is an endangered species, it also has the healthiest population numbers-wise and the population is considered decreasing at the moment.


Siberian Tigers

Siberian Tigers are native to the Russian far east, Northeast China, and possibly North Korea. The Siberian is the only Tiger that lives in a snowy habitat, and is genetically close to the now-extinct Caspian Tiger. Siberian Tigers are on average the second largest of the subspecies, however some males have been known to grow to a size of 675 lbs. Their coats are a rusty reddish color, with narrow black stripes across its body. A Siberian's coat is moderately thick, helping with the colder weather of Eastern Russia. They are also endangered and have a population of around 400 or more in the wild with stable numbers.


Sumatran Tigers

Sumatran Tigers are found fragmented across Sumatra, and are considered some of the smallest Tigers in terms of both height and weight. Although all tigers possess a strong ability to swim and enjoy swimming, Sumatran Tigers are considered the best swimmers alongside the Bengal due to adaptations to their enviroment. They live mainly within the rainforests of Sumatra and have a population of roughly 400-600 in the wild, considered critically endangered with a decreasing population similar to the Indochinese and Malayan Tigers.


Indochinese Tigers

Indochinese Tigers are native to Southeast Asia, specifically within Myanmar and Thailand, and possibly a very small numbers withing Vietnam. They live within tropical forests and hover around a population of 250, putting them at critically endangered status with a decreasing population. The Indochinese Tigers colouration is darker, with more rather short and narrow stripes. They are very good at utilizing camouflage, helping them with hunting their prey.


Malayan Tigers

Malayan Tigers are native to the southern and central parts of the Malayasian Peninsula, and outside of the South China Tiger, is the smallest in numbers sitting at only 200 left in the wild, with an estimated 80 to 120 of those being mature adults. They are also Critically endangered with a steady decline in population due to development projects and agriculture, primarily involving palm oil production. There aren't many clear difference between the Malayan Tiger and the Indochinese Tiger, the former being on the smaller size at roughly half the height of other tigers, and on the lighter end in terms of weight.


South China Tigers

South China Tigers were native to Southern China, however they are currently listed as Critically Endangered or even possibly extinct within the wild as the last confirmed sighting within the wild was back in the 1980's. Since the late 1990's continued survival for any potential survivors was considered extremely unlikely due to numerous amounts of factors such as prey density, widespread habitat degradation, and other environmental issues. Unfortunately despite efforts from local sanctuaries and zoos, the population even within captivity is still only around 100 individuals. As of this time there are attempts to "rewild" the population back into South Africa. The South China Tiger is also considered to be the most ancient subspecies of tiger which all other subspecies descend from.
